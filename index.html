GLOBAL DEATH RATE IN 2019

INTRODUCTION

The question of what causes people to lose their lives is of paramount importance, guiding critical decisions in public health and ultimately saving lives. Sadly, many of the leading causes of death often remain in the shadows, receiving insufficient public attention. Over time, the death rates stemming from these causes have witnessed significant incline on a global scale. Hence, it is crucial to take into a deeper understanding of the root casues of mortality that fall across the age group.



HOW MANY PEOPLE DIE IN 2019?

Overall, the total number of deaths in 2019 was 50.7 million, with a global death rate of 723.9 per 100,000 people. The highest death rate was observed in Bulgaria, at 2539.9 per 100,000 people, while the lowest was reported in Qatar, at 170.6 per 100,000 people. By averaging the death rates across all countries, we find that the global average death rate was 741.8 per 100,000 people. This highlights the significant number of deaths that occur worldwide, underscoring the need for targeted interventions to reduce preventable deaths.

ENVIRONMENTAL FACTOR VS DEATH RATE IN 2019


From the environmental perspective, we find that the predominant cause of death is outdoor air pollution, accounting for approximately 6400 deaths per 100,000 people in 2019, which is the highest cumulative death rate observed across all continents. On the other hand, unsafe water sources causes the lowest death rate, at 2550 deaths per 100,000 people, which is 2.5 times lower than that of outdoor air pollution. Air pollution from solid fuels and secondhand smoke also contribute to the total death rate, at approximately 4963 and 2684 per 100,000 people respectively.

NUTRITIONAL HEALTH VS DEATH RATE IN 2019


Let's now shift our focus to the nutritional factors that influence death rates.


From the bubble plot on the right, it is obvious that there is a positive correlation (R2 = 0.56) between death rates due to high systolic blood pressure and diets high in sodium. Notably, the size of the data points increases proportionally with higher death rates associated with both high systolic blood pressure and high sodium intake. This suggests a strong likelihood that individuals who succumb to high glucose consumption are also at risk of succumbing to high systolic blood pressure.


Zooming in on specific countries, such as Malaysia and Australia, further illuminates the disparities. Malaysia exhibits a higher death rate attributed to both high systolic blood pressure and high sodium diets compared to Australia. Additionally, Malaysia reports a slightly higher death rate due to high fasting plasma glucose levels than Australia.

HOW DOES DIET AFFECT DEATH RATE?


Now, let's take a closer look at the relationship between diet and death rates. The bar chart on the left illustrates the top 10 countries with the highest death rates attributed to diets low in plant-based sources in 2019. Plant-based sources include whole grains, fruits, nuts and seeds, as well as vegetables.


Surprisingly, more than half of the countries on this list are located in Europe, with Ukraine claiming the top spot with a notably high average death rate of 288.65 per 100,000 people. It is worth noting that the global average death rate in this context stands at 57.37 per 100,000 people, putting these figures into perspective and highlighting the significance of dietary choices in influencing health outcomes worldwide.





WHAT PLANT-BASED DIETS ARE PEOPLE LACKING OF?


When examining diet practices on a global scale, it becomes evident that diets low in vegetables tend to be associated with higher death rates compared to other dietary habits. In contrast, diets low in nuts and seeds appear to have lower death rates.


Now, let's narrow our focus to the Asia and Oceania continents. Here, we observe a consistent pattern: low vegetable consumption is consistently linked to higher death rates, while diets low in nuts and seeds are associated with the lowest death rates. Interestingly, low diet in fruit and low diet in whole grain show similar effects on death rates in both of these continents.



LIFESTYLE FACTOR VS DEATH RATE IN 2019


The waffle chart provides a visual representation of how different lifestyle behaviors influenced death rates across continents in 2019. Notably, Europe has the highest contribution to the total death rate at 46%, primarily due to smoking. This underscores the significant health impact of smoking in the region. In contrast, South America accounts for only 4% of the total death rate, the lowest among all continents.


Digging deeper, Europe also leads in death rates related to drug and alcohol use. Surprisingly, Oceania stands apart with the lowest death rate due to alcohol use, at just 4%.


Taking a closer look at Asia, we find a variety of health challenges. Smoking contributes to 19% of deaths, drug use to 21%, and alcohol use to 15%. This highlights the diverse health issues within the continent.




SUMMARY

This exploration delves into the profound influence of distinct risk factor categories on death rates, with a specific focus on environmental, nutritional, and lifestyle factors. Environmental risks, encompassing factors like air pollution and unsafe water sources, have the potential to contribute significantly to elevated mortality rates. Nutritional factors, particularly the adoption of balanced diets, play a pivotal role in preventing waterborne diseases and ensuring overall health. Furthermore, lifestyle choices, including habits like smoking, drug use, and alcohol addiction, exert a substantial impact on mortality rates. This deep understanding of how these factors are interconnected provides us with valuable insights to develop effective strategies in public health for reducing preventable deaths.
